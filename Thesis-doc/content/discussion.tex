\section{Verdicts on H1--H3}
\label{sec:Verdicts}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{H1 (XPBD degrades under high mass ratios) ---
\emph{TODO: confirmed / refuted}.}
Tied to \autoref{ssec:MassRatioPendulum} and \autoref{ssec:MassRatioChain}.
Mechanism (if confirmed): dual updates propagate slowly through chains of
strongly mismatched masses because each Gauss--Seidel sweep only changes
one constraint's multiplier, and the change couples to the next constraint
only through the position update on the shared particle.

\paragraph{H2 (VBD degrades under high stiffness ratios) ---
\emph{TODO: confirmed / refuted}.}
Tied to \autoref{ssec:StiffnessRatioChain} and \autoref{ssec:StiffRatioTest}.
Mechanism (if confirmed): the local Hessian block $\mathbf{H}_{i}$ at a
vertex sums contributions from every incident spring, so when the
stiffnesses vary widely the block's condition number explodes, slowing the
Newton step.

\paragraph{H3 (VBD's per-iteration convergence is more stiffness-robust)
--- \emph{TODO: confirmed / refuted}.}
Tied to \autoref{ssec:ResidualConvergence} and \autoref{ssec:IterIndepStiff}.
Mechanism (if confirmed): VBD uses second-order information about $E$
locally, so increasing $k$ does not change the number of Newton steps to
convergence very much; XPBD discards this information.

\paragraph{Joint reading.}
If H1, H2, H3 all hold, the framing is consistent: each solver is robust
in exactly the regime where its update direction sees the right
information, and brittle in the regime where the other side of the dual
would have. The practical recommendation in such a case is regime-based,
not absolute --- which is itself a useful contribution if the experiments
support it.

\section{Threats to validity}
\label{sec:ThreatsValidity}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Single-threaded execution}
\label{ssec:ThreatSingleThread}
All measurements run single-threaded on CPU. VBD is known to parallelise
better than XPBD (\autoref{sec:Parallelisation}), so ms/frame results
underestimate VBD's likely production advantage. This affects external
validity of any quantitative efficiency claim but does not invalidate
qualitative claims about convergence quality or stability envelopes.

\subsection{Mass-spring discretisation only}
\label{ssec:ThreatMassSpring}
I use distance-spring energies throughout, including for bending. Real
cloth simulators use FEM-based stretch energies and dihedral bending. The
primal/dual framing carries over (both XPBD and VBD admit arbitrary elastic
energies), but the specific stiffness ratios encountered, and therefore
the magnitude of the H2/H3 effects, may differ.

\subsection{Gauss--Seidel order dependence}
\label{ssec:ThreatOrder}
Both solvers are order-sensitive (\autoref{sec:GSJacobi}). I fix a single
deterministic order; an adversarial reordering could in principle shift
results. I do not investigate this, and it is a real threat to
reproducibility on other implementations.

\subsection{Mismatched collision models}
\label{ssec:ThreatCollision}
XPBD uses projection-based self-collision; VBD uses penalty-based
self-collision (\autoref{sec:SelfCollision}). Any difference in the
self-collision experiments mixes algorithmic and contact-model effects.

\subsection{Fixed $dt = 1/24$~s}
\label{ssec:ThreatDt}
The choice $dt = 1/24$~s is generous for Unity but stresses both solvers.
A different fixed $dt$, or an adaptive step, could shift the equal-wall-
clock frontier. The stability map in \autoref{ssec:StabilityMap} mitigates
this by characterising both solvers over a $dt$ range, but the headline
results in \autoref{sec:EffResults} are tied to a single $dt$.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:Limitations}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Beyond the threats above, three further limitations are worth naming
explicitly:
\begin{itemize}
    \item I do not implement AVBD or any post-2024 VBD extension.
    \item Self-collision uses a simple model; production simulators often
    use continuous collision detection (CCD), which both solvers can be
    augmented with at additional cost.
    \item Friction is not modelled; the experiments are friction-free.
\end{itemize}
