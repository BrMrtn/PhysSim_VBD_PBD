\paragraph{Mass-ratio regime.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Tied to \autoref{ssec:MassRatioPendulum} and \autoref{ssec:MassRatioChain}.
    \item Interpretation: dual updates propagate slowly through chains of strongly mismatched masses, because each Gauss--Seidel sweep changes only one constraint's multiplier, and the change couples to the next constraint only through the position update on the shared particle. \emph{TODO: state what the measurements actually show here.}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Stiffness-ratio regime.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Tied to \autoref{ssec:StiffnessRatioChain} and \autoref{ssec:StiffRatioTest}.
    \item Interpretation: the local Hessian block $\mathbf{H}_{i}$ at a vertex sums contributions from every incident spring, so widely varying stiffnesses inflate the block's condition number and can slow the Newton step.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Per-iteration convergence under stiffness.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Tied to \autoref{ssec:ResidualConvergence} and \autoref{ssec:IterIndepStiff}.
    \item Interpretation: VBD uses second-order information about $E$ locally, so increasing $k$ need not change the number of Newton steps to convergence much, whereas XPBD discards this information.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Joint reading.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Read together, the three regimes describe where each solver's update direction sees the information it needs and where it does not.
    \item Where the results bear this out, the practical recommendation is regime-based rather than absolute --- a useful outcome in its own right. \emph{TODO: finalise once results are in.}
\end{itemize}

\section{Threats to validity}
\label{sec:ThreatsValidity}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Single-threaded execution}
\label{ssec:ThreatSingleThread}
\begin{itemize}
    \item All measurements run single-threaded on CPU.
    \item VBD is known to parallelise better than XPBD (\autoref{sec:Parallelisation}), so ms/frame results underestimate VBD's likely production advantage.
    \item This affects external validity of any quantitative efficiency claim but does not invalidate qualitative claims about convergence quality or stability envelopes.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Mass-spring discretisation only}
\label{ssec:ThreatMassSpring}
\begin{itemize}
    \item I use distance-spring energies throughout, including for bending; real cloth simulators use FEM-based stretch energies and dihedral bending.
    \item The primal/dual framing carries over (both XPBD and VBD admit arbitrary elastic energies), but the specific stiffness ratios encountered --- and therefore the magnitude of the stiffness-ratio and per-iteration-convergence effects --- may differ.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Gauss--Seidel order dependence}
\label{ssec:ThreatOrder}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Both solvers are order-sensitive (\autoref{sec:GSJacobi}).
    \item I fix a single deterministic order; an adversarial reordering could in principle shift results.
    \item I do not investigate this, and it is a real threat to reproducibility on other implementations.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Fixed $dt = 1/30$~s}
\label{ssec:ThreatDt}
\begin{itemize}
    \item $dt = 1/30$~s is generous for Unity but stresses both solvers.
    \item A different fixed $dt$, or an adaptive step, could shift the equal-wall-clock frontier.
    \item The stability map in \autoref{ssec:StabilityMap} mitigates this by characterising both solvers over a $dt$ range, but the headline results in \autoref{sec:EffResults} are tied to a single $dt$.
\end{itemize}
