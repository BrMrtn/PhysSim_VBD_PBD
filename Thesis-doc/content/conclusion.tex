\section{Summary}
\label{sec:Summary}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
This thesis presented a controlled comparison of XPBD --- one of the most
established solvers for real-time deformable simulation --- and VBD, a primal
alternative introduced in 2024. The contribution is threefold: a shared Unity
codebase that implements both solvers under matched material parameters and a
matched compute budget; a Newton ground-truth reference that enables reporting
absolute solution error rather than only relative performance; and an evaluation
along four axes --- efficiency, robustness, physical realism, and flexibility ---
read against the primal/dual distinction that motivated the stress tests.

The headline finding is that neither solver dominates; each wins in the regime
its update direction is suited to. On the equal-wall-clock frontier VBD leads
when the per-step problem is hard (large time steps), while XPBD overtakes it
once finer substepping makes each sub-problem easy enough to converge in one or
two cheap iterations. Robustness splits exactly as the primal/dual lens predicts:
XPBD, the dual solver, handles extreme stiffness ratios and near-rigid
constraints but stalls under a $1000\times$ mass ratio; VBD, the primal solver,
handles the mass ratio but becomes unstable under the wide stiffness ratios of a
realistic cloth model unless its vertex mass is tuned. At sufficient iterations
both reach the Newton reference and are physically indistinguishable, so realism
reduces to a per-iteration-quality question already captured by the efficiency
axis. On flexibility, XPBD is easier to extend with scalar constraints and
benefits from a decade of deployment across many domains, whereas VBD's
per-vertex structure is heavier to extend but parallelises more cleanly. The
practical recommendation is therefore scenario-based rather than absolute, and is
set out in full in \autoref{sec:Verdicts}.

\section{Future work}
\label{sec:FutureWork}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Multithreaded and GPU comparison}
\label{ssec:FWParallel}
The most consequential extension is to repeat the experiments on a multithreaded
CPU and on the GPU, directly lifting the single-threaded limitation of
\autoref{ssec:ThreatSingleThread}. Both solvers admit parallelization through
graph coloring, but their structures differ in granularity
(\autoref{sec:Parallelisation}): VBD's per-vertex blocks should color more cleanly
than XPBD's per-constraint blocks. The expected outcome is therefore that
parallelization widens VBD's lead, and quantifying that gap is the natural next
study.

\subsection{Extending the research to other domains of dynamics}
\label{ssec:FWOtherDynamics}
Beyond mass-spring systems, both solvers could in theory also handle other cloth
representations, soft bodies, and rigid bodies. It would be valuable to see how the
two perform on these other kinds of dynamics, and whether the same conclusions
continue to hold.

\subsection{Using a different cloth model}
\label{ssec:FWFEM}
The mass-spring discretization is the source of the bias discussed in
\autoref{ssec:ThreatMassSpring}, which makes the cloth model the most informative
ingredient to vary next. The cheapest change is to replace my
twelve-neighbor-per-vertex model with a four- or eight-neighbor one; this narrows
the range of representable materials but would reveal how sensitive the comparison
is to mesh connectivity. A more substantial change is to swap the mass-spring
energy for an energy-based model such as Saint-Venant--Kirchhoff (StVK), the model
the original VBD paper uses; I would expect it to favor XPBD far less. A
particularly informative variant would pair the models asymmetrically ---
mass-spring XPBD against StVK VBD --- letting each solver run on the discretization
that suits it best.

\subsection{AVBD and post-2024 variants}
\label{ssec:FWAVBD}
Augmented VBD~\cite{Giles2025} reportedly handles wide stiffness ratios and high
stiffness substantially better than canonical VBD. Re-running the stiffness-ratio
experiments with AVBD would resolve the question left open in
\autoref{ssec:ThreatAVBD}: whether the gap I observe is intrinsic to the primal
approach or an artifact of the canonical 2024 implementation.
