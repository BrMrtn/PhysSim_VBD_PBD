\chapter{Experiments and results}
\label{chap:Experiments}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter reports measurements along the four evaluation axes introduced in
\autoref{sec:EvalAxes}: efficiency, robustness, physical realism, and
flexibility. Unless stated otherwise, every experiment uses the shared
scaffolding and fairness controls of \autoref{sec:Fairness}: matched gravity,
$dt = 1/30$~s, no damping, $\alpha_{\mathrm{XPBD}} = 1/k_{\mathrm{VBD}}$, and
Chebyshev acceleration disabled in VBD for the core comparison.

\subsection{Influence of substep and iteration count on error and compute time}
\label{ssec:ErrorVsMs}

For each solver I sweep over a range of substep and iteration configurations and
plot the Pareto front of the resulting runs --- the set of configurations not
dominated by any other, meaning that no run is simultaneously faster and more
accurate --- using average ms/frame against the average root-mean-squared
position error measured against a Newton reference. Both solvers are swept over
every combination of the substep and iteration counts
$n_{\mathrm{sub}} \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32\}$ and
$n_{\mathrm{it}} \in \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32\}$. For this problem the iteration count
is enforced within VBD (no early stopping), to keep the comparison clean. Each
diagram in \autoref{fig:ErrorVsMs} uses a Newton reference computed with a
different number of substeps.

The test problem is a chain of ten nodes with one end pinned and a weight of 100
at the free end. The nodes are connected in sequence, one to the next, with no
other constraints between them. Initially the free end is raised to the same
height as the pinned end and positioned so that the chain is neither stretched nor
compressed, and only gravity acts on the system.

My results show that a single VBD iteration always approximates the Newton
solution more closely than a single XPBD iteration, but it is also more
expensive. Especially against the lower-iteration references (1 and 4 substeps),
VBD usually achieves better performance by increasing its iteration count,
whereas XPBD's best solutions instead come from increasing the number of
substeps. This is clearest in the diagram with the 1-substep Newton reference,
where several of XPBD's best solutions come from raising the substep count rather
than the iteration count. For harder problems --- where the time step is large
enough, with fewer substeps --- VBD takes the lead. As the reference becomes more
refined with more substeps, the sub-problems become easy enough for both solvers
to converge in one or two iterations, and at that point XPBD takes over thanks to
its cheaper per-iteration cost.

\subsection{Frame time vs.\ node count}
\label{ssec:NodeCountVsFPS}

To measure how each solver scales with problem size, I record the average frames
per second on a hanging-cloth scene with wind interaction, at mesh resolutions
from $8\times 8$ to $128\times 128$, holding the solver settings fixed. The
primary comparison is XPBD with 4 substeps and 4 iterations
($XPBD_{sub4\times it4}$) against VBD with 4 substeps and a maximum of 4
iterations ($VBD_{sub4\times it_{\max}4}$), which gives the ``same parameter''
comparison. To see how much VBD gains from early stopping, I also include VBD
variants with enforced iteration counts: $VBD_{sub4\times it4}$, which shows how
the two algorithms compare with truly matching parameters, and
$VBD_{sub4\times it2}$, which the Pareto fronts of \autoref{ssec:ErrorVsMs}
suggest performs close to $XPBD_{sub4\times it4}$ in both ``realism'' and
``efficiency.''

In my tests, $VBD_{sub4\times it2}$ always outperforms $XPBD_{sub4\times it4}$,
while $VBD_{sub4\times it4}$ always trails it slightly. As expected,
$VBD_{sub4\times it_{\max}4}$ provides a fair middle ground, outperforming XPBD on
the easier problems and converging toward its own enforced variant on the harder
ones. \autoref{fig:NodeCountVsFPS} shows a $32\times 32$-node cloth in this
experiment's test environment as it progresses in time.

\label{ssec:ChainWithHeavyWeight}

This scene is a chain fixed at one end, with the particle at the other end given

\subsection{Chain with high stiffness ratio}
\label{ssec:StiffnessRatioChain}

This scene is a chain of alternating soft and stiff springs, with a stiffness
ratio of $1{:}10000$, mirroring the experiment in the original VBD
paper~\cite{Chen2024}. As in the previous experiment, all three methods in
\autoref{fig:StiffnessRatioChain} use one substep and 100 iterations. The roles
are now reversed: VBD --- a primal solver --- does not converge to the reference
solution, while XPBD matches it almost perfectly.

\subsection{Self-collision: cloth folded onto itself}
\label{ssec:SelfCollisionDrop}

Both solvers can also be used to simulate collisions. In this scene I drop a cloth
onto the ground so that it folds onto itself. As noted in
\autoref{sec:SelfCollision}, the two solvers implement self-collision
differently, so rather than give exact measurements I report my observations.

Physically accurate cloth requires handling many stiff constraints: woven
fabrics, for instance, stretch by only about 5--10\%. The cloth model of
\autoref{ssec:ClothModel} attaches twelve springs of three different stiffness
values to each interior node, and those values can differ greatly --- exactly
the setup that the stiffness-ratio experiment above shows to be a serious
disadvantage for VBD. The result is very unstable behavior:
even when the simulation does not blow up, the cloth overshoots most
configurations. Initial vertex noise on the scale of $0.001$ produces large
movements --- visible in the first image, where the cloth changes shape even
before colliding with the ground --- and the cloth keeps shifting along the floor
even after it appears to have settled.

The instability can be tamed by giving the vertices a large enough mass; my
experiments suggest the vertex mass needs to be at minimum within two to three
orders of magnitude of the largest stiffness value. This is exactly why the
original VBD paper~\cite{Chen2024} uses a different cloth model, and it shows that
VBD sometimes has to be hand-tuned to stay stable. VBD also cannot handle
infinitely stiff constraints. AVBD~\cite{Giles2025} improves substantially on this
axis and is more robust, but between canonical VBD and XPBD, XPBD is the more
robust solver for this problem.

\autoref{fig:SelfCollisionW1} and \autoref{fig:SelfCollisionW1e4} show the drop at
a total cloth mass of 1 and of 10000, respectively; in each, the top row is XPBD
and the bottom row is VBD.

\subsection{Harmonic oscillator}
\label{ssec:HarmonicOscillator}

The harmonic oscillator is implemented as a single distance constraint between a
pinned top node and a free bottom node, and I measure its amplitude over time. To
compare against Fig.~2 of the XPBD paper~\cite{10.1145/2994258.2994272}, I use the
same parameters, only with the direction reversed: rest length 1, compliance
$\alpha = 10^{-3}$, rest position $x = -1$, initial position $x_0 = -0.5$, and mass
$m = 1$.

Regardless of $dt$, both solvers converge in a single iteration and follow the
same reference curve perfectly. \autoref{fig:Oscillator} also shows the analytic solution
$x(t) = x_0\cos(\omega t)$ with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m} = 31.62~\mathrm{s}^{-1}$ as
dashed lines. As in the XPBD example, all of the visible damping is a
side effect of the implicit discretization of the equations of motion.

\subsection{Ease of adding a new functionality}
\label{ssec:NewFunctionality}

To compare how easily each solver can be extended, I had Claude Code (Opus 4.7) implement
dihedral bending constraints in both. Both methods already include this
constraint in their original papers (XPBD~\cite{10.1145/2994258.2994272}, VBD~\cite{Chen2024}), so I follow those implementations. The
existing bending constraint is a distance constraint acting as a skip-spring
between two non-adjacent nodes; the task was to implement the dihedral version and
make bending toggleable between the dihedral and distance formulations.
\autoref{tab:Extension} summarizes the effort.

The two implementations share a new $\approx$30-LOC file that computes the
gradients from the four hinge-node positions, and the in-solver addition that
solves the new constraint is about 28 LOC and essentially identical in both. XPBD
was easier to extend: the new constraint resembles the existing distance
constraint (but on four particles instead of two), introduces no new concept, and
plugs in through a single extra \texttt{SolveDihedralConstraints} call without
changing any existing code, with each hinge visited once per iteration. VBD needed
more setup code because it is vertex-based: each hinge has to be stored four times
--- once per incident vertex --- and its data type carries an extra parameter
naming which of the four vertices is currently being processed. The hinge Hessian
block is therefore recomputed four times per iteration, which makes the VBD
extension roughly four times as expensive as the XPBD one. Mathematically, XPBD
needs only the first derivative of the constraint, whereas VBD needs the second
derivative, which is arguably harder to derive and implement.

In general, for scalar constraints --- which quantify a condition with a single
numerical value, such as a distance or an angle --- XPBD is the easier solver to
extend.

\subsection{Coverage of different physical domains}
\label{ssec:DomainCoverage}

PBD has been around for almost 20 years and XPBD for about 10. These methods have
become industry standards for in-game physics. They have been implemented across
many domains --- cloth, soft bodies, rigid bodies, fluids, hair --- with a wide
range of constraint types and forces. As a dual solver, XPBD copes well with a
wide range of stiffness values, including very stiff and near-rigid constraints
(as the stiffness-ratio experiment in \autoref{ssec:StiffnessRatioChain} shows),
which together with its large audience makes it a very flexible solver. This also
matched my experience in the test environment: XPBD withstood a wide range of
parameters without breaking down, and generally worked out of the box.

VBD, by contrast, is a very new method, published in 2024, and is not yet widely
used; its original paper applies it mostly to elastic body dynamics. As the
self-collision experiment showed, it is not well suited to very hard (effectively
infinite-stiffness) constraints --- an issue that AVBD addresses by bringing
rigid bodies into the method. In my test environment VBD often had to be tuned
--- adjusting stiffness ratios or vertex mass relative to stiffness, as discussed in
\autoref{ssec:SelfCollisionDrop} --- to stay stable, whereas XPBD tolerated the
same configurations without such hand-tuning.
