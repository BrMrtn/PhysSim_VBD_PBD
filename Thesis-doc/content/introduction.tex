\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:Motivation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Real-time simulation of deformable objects --- cloth, soft bodies, hair --- has
become a baseline expectation in interactive applications such as games,
virtual reality and augmented reality. In these domains the simulator must
deliver believable dynamics inside an extremely tight frame budget: at
60~fps the entire physics, rendering and gameplay logic for a frame must
complete in roughly 16~ms, with physics typically receiving only a
fraction of that.

We focus on deformable bodies rather than rigid bodies for two reasons.
First, the two algorithms studied here differ most visibly on deformable
material: rigid-body simulation hides the per-constraint convergence
behaviour that we want to measure. Second, deformable simulation is the
strictly harder case --- many more degrees of freedom and many more
constraints per frame --- so it stresses both solvers in ways that make
their failure modes legible.

\paragraph{The accuracy/efficiency trade-off.}
Every physics-based simulator sits somewhere on a line between
\emph{physical accuracy} and \emph{computational efficiency}: gaining one
costs the other. \autoref{tab:accuracy-efficiency} sketches this spectrum
for the methods relevant to this thesis, ordered from most accurate to
most efficient. XPBD and VBD both sit near the efficient end --- which is
where real-time applications live --- and the rest of the thesis asks
which of the two makes the better trade-off, and under what conditions.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{l l l}
\toprule
\textbf{Method} & \textbf{Accuracy} & \textbf{Cost} \\
\midrule
Newton's method (implicit Euler)   & reference            & offline only \\
Projective Dynamics                & high                 & expensive    \\
Vertex Block Descent (VBD)         & moderate--high       & real-time    \\
Extended Position-Based Dynamics (XPBD) & moderate         & real-time    \\
Position-Based Dynamics (PBD)      & low (stiffness-dep.) & cheap        \\
Explicit Euler                     & poor (unstable)      & cheapest     \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Deformable-simulation methods ordered from most accurate to most
efficient. XPBD and VBD --- the subject of this thesis --- both target the
real-time regime.}
\label{tab:accuracy-efficiency}
\end{table}

Designing such a simulator is hard for three reasons that recur throughout
this thesis:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Stiffness.} Cloth and soft tissue exhibit very stiff
    constraints (almost-inextensible fabric, near-rigid bones). Stiff systems
    drive explicit integrators unstable unless time steps are made
    impractically small.
    \item \emph{Stability.} Even implicit integrators can blow up when
    constraints are poorly conditioned, when mass ratios between connected
    particles are large, or when contact events introduce sudden velocity
    discontinuities.
    \item \emph{Strict time constraints.} Methods that converge well in offline
    animation (Newton's method, projective dynamics) are typically too
    expensive per frame; methods cheap enough for real-time often trade
    convergence quality for predictable cost.
\end{itemize}

Within the real-time band, Extended Position-Based Dynamics
(XPBD)~\cite{10.1145/2994258.2994272} has been a workhorse for years, while
Vertex Block Descent (VBD)~\cite{Chen2024} is a recent and promising
addition that has yet to be evaluated head-to-head against it. The two
methods occupy opposite sides of the \emph{primal/dual} duality identified
by Macklin et~al.~\cite{Chen2024}: XPBD solves for Lagrange multipliers
(the dual variables associated with constraints), while VBD solves
directly for vertex positions (the primal variables). No controlled
comparison between them currently exists.

\section{Contribution}
\label{sec:Contribution}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
The central contribution of this thesis is, to the best of our knowledge, the
\emph{first faithful XPBD-vs-VBD comparison}: the two algorithms are
implemented in the same Unity-based codebase, applied to the same mass-spring
material model, run under a matched compute budget, and evaluated against a
high-iteration Newton solve that serves as a numerical ground-truth reference.

This sharpens previous work. The closest prior comparison, Ma
et~al.~\cite{app142311072}, contrasts \emph{plain} PBD (not XPBD) with a
non-canonical VBD implementation, and reports only frame-rate scaling. We
distinguish ourselves by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item using XPBD, the constraint-stiffness-independent variant that any
    practitioner would actually deploy today;
    \item implementing the canonical VBD formulation, including its
    block-coordinate-descent interpretation;
    \item matching compute budgets and material parameters rather than
    iteration counts, since one XPBD substep is not directly comparable to
    one VBD iteration;
    \item adding a Newton ground truth, which lets us report
    \emph{solution error} rather than only relative performance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Hypotheses}
\label{sec:Hypotheses}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
The primal/dual theory in \autoref{chap:Background} predicts that XPBD and
VBD should fail in characteristically different regimes. We state three
testable hypotheses up front; the experiments in \autoref{chap:Experiments}
are designed to confirm or refute them:
\begin{description}
    \item[H1.] \emph{XPBD degrades under high mass ratios.} Because XPBD
    solves dual variables one constraint at a time, large mass disparities
    along a constraint chain amplify the local update error and propagate
    slowly through the system.
    \item[H2.] \emph{VBD degrades under high stiffness ratios.} VBD's primal
    block-coordinate update relies on a locally well-conditioned Hessian; a
    wide range of stiffnesses meeting at a single vertex produces an
    ill-conditioned block and slows convergence.
    \item[H3.] \emph{VBD's per-iteration convergence is more
    stiffness-robust.} Because the primal update sees the true elastic
    Hessian rather than a linearised constraint, raising material stiffness
    should not slow VBD as much as it slows XPBD.
\end{description}

\section{Evaluation axes}
\label{sec:EvalAxes}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
We evaluate both solvers along four axes that, together, capture the
trade-offs a practitioner cares about:
\begin{description}
    \item[Efficiency.] Wall-clock cost per frame as a function of mesh size,
    substep/iteration count, and target quality.
    \item[Robustness.] Behaviour under stress: large time steps, single
    iterations, extreme mass ratios, extreme stiffness ratios, self-contact.
    \item[Physical realism.] Distance from a Newton reference, energy
    behaviour, damping behaviour, and agreement with analytic solutions on
    a harmonic oscillator and a draped cloth.
    \item[Flexibility.] Ease of changing material stiffness, adding new
    constraint types, and reasoning about parameters.
\end{description}

\section{Thesis structure}
\label{sec:Structure}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\autoref{chap:Background} develops the theoretical foundation: the variational
form of implicit Euler, mass-spring energies, and the primal/dual lens that
makes XPBD and VBD comparable. \autoref{chap:RelatedWork} reviews the PBD,
XPBD, and VBD literature and positions this work against Ma
et~al.~\cite{app142311072}. \autoref{chap:Algorithms} states the two
algorithms in their canonical form. \autoref{chap:Methodology} describes the
Unity implementation and, importantly, the design decisions that keep the
comparison fair. \autoref{chap:Experiments} reports results along the four
axes. \autoref{chap:Discussion} synthesises across axes, judges H1--H3, and
lists threats to validity. \autoref{chap:Conclusion} closes with directions
for future work.
