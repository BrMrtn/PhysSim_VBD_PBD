\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:Motivation}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{itemize}
    \item Real-time simulation is a baseline expectation in games, VR, and AR.
    \item Elastic body dynamics (strings, cloth, soft bodies)
    \item The simulator must deliver believable dynamics within a tight frame budget: at 60~fps a frame allows $\sim$16~ms for all physics, rendering, and gameplay logic, with physics getting only a fraction.
    \item I focus on deformable bodies rather than rigid bodies because:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item the two studied algorithms differ most visibly on deformable material --- rigid-body simulation hides the per-constraint convergence behaviour I want to measure;
        \item deformable simulation is the strictly harder case (many more degrees of freedom and constraints per frame), stressing both solvers so their failure modes become legible.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{The accuracy/efficiency trade-off.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Every physics-based simulator sits on a line between \emph{physical accuracy} and \emph{computational efficiency}; gaining one costs the other.
    \item \autoref{tab:accuracy-efficiency} sketches this spectrum, ordered from most accurate to most efficient.
    \item XPBD and VBD both sit near the efficient end --- where real-time applications live --- and the thesis asks which makes the better trade-off, and under what conditions.
\end{itemize}

Designing such a simulator is hard for three recurring reasons:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Stiffness.} Cloth and soft tissue have very stiff constraints (almost-inextensible fabric, near-rigid bones); stiff systems make explicit integrators unstable unless time steps become impractically small.
    \item \emph{Stability.} Even implicit integrators can blow up under poorly conditioned constraints, large mass ratios between connected particles, or contact-induced velocity discontinuities.
    \item \emph{Strict time constraints.} Methods that converge well offline (Newton's method, projective dynamics) are too expensive per frame; cheap real-time methods often trade convergence quality for predictable cost.
\end{itemize}

Within the real-time band:
\begin{itemize}
    \item XPBD~\cite{10.1145/2994258.2994272} has been a workhorse for years.
    \item VBD~\cite{Chen2024} is a recent, promising addition not yet evaluated head-to-head against XPBD.
    \item The two occupy opposite sides of the \emph{primal/dual} duality identified by Macklin et~al.~\cite{10.1111/cgf.14104}: XPBD solves for Lagrange multipliers (dual variables on constraints), VBD solves directly for vertex positions (primal variables).
    \item No controlled comparison between them currently exists.
\end{itemize}

\section{Contribution}
\label{sec:Contribution}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{itemize}
    \item The central contribution is, to the best of my knowledge, the \emph{first faithful XPBD-vs-VBD comparison}:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item both algorithms implemented in the same Unity-based codebase,
        \item applied to the same mass-spring material model,
        \item run under a matched compute budget,
        \item evaluated against a high-iteration Newton solve serving as numerical ground-truth reference.
    \end{itemize}
    \item This sharpens the closest prior comparison, Ma et~al.~\cite{app142311072}, which contrasts \emph{plain} PBD (not XPBD) with a non-canonical VBD implementation and reports only frame-rate scaling.
\end{itemize}

I distinguish this work by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item using XPBD, the constraint-stiffness-independent variant any practitioner would deploy today;
    \item implementing the canonical VBD formulation, including its block-coordinate-descent interpretation;
    \item matching compute budgets and material parameters rather than iteration counts (one XPBD substep is not directly comparable to one VBD iteration);
    \item adding a Newton ground truth, enabling reporting of \emph{solution error} rather than only relative performance.
\end{enumerate}

\section{The primal/dual lens}
\label{sec:PrimalDualLens}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{itemize}
    \item XPBD and VBD sit on opposite sides of the primal/dual split developed in \autoref{chap:Background}: XPBD updates dual variables one constraint at a time, VBD updates primal positions one vertex at a time.
    \item This split is the lens I use to decide what is worth measuring. A dual solver is sensitive to how information propagates along chains of coupled constraints, so mass disparities along such a chain are a natural stress; a primal solver is sensitive to the conditioning of each per-vertex block, so a wide spread of stiffnesses meeting at one vertex is a natural stress. The experiments in \autoref{chap:Experiments} are organised around exactly these regimes.
    \item The goal is to map where each solver is accurate, stable, and cheap, and where it is brittle --- not to argue for the primal/dual framing itself, which I take as given from \cite{10.1111/cgf.14104}.
\end{itemize}

\section{Evaluation axes}
\label{sec:EvalAxes}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
I evaluate both solvers along four axes that together capture the trade-offs a practitioner cares about:
\begin{description}
    \item[Efficiency.] Wall-clock cost per frame as a function of mesh size, substep/iteration count, and target quality.
    \item[Robustness.] Behaviour under stress: large time steps, single iterations, extreme mass ratios, extreme stiffness ratios, self-contact.
    \item[Physical realism.] Distance from a Newton reference, energy behaviour, damping behaviour, and agreement with analytic solutions on a harmonic oscillator and a draped cloth.
    \item[Flexibility.] Ease of changing material stiffness, adding new constraint types, and reasoning about parameters.
\end{description}
