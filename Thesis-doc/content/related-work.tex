\chapter{Related work}
\label{chap:RelatedWork}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter surveys the position-based and primal-descent lineages whose
endpoints --- XPBD and VBD --- this thesis compares, and positions our work
relative to the closest prior comparison.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{From PBD to XPBD}
\label{sec:PBDtoXPBD}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Position-Based Dynamics (PBD) was introduced by M\"uller
et~al.~\cite{MULLER2007109} for real-time cloth and soft-body simulation in
games. Each iteration projects positions onto constraint manifolds (e.g.\
fixed edge lengths) via Gauss--Seidel sweeps, recovering velocities by
finite difference. PBD is unconditionally stable but suffers a well-known
flaw: \emph{constraint stiffness depends on iteration count and time step}.
Practitioners cannot specify a Young's modulus directly; they specify a
stiffness coefficient that interacts unpredictably with solver settings.

Extended Position-Based Dynamics (XPBD), introduced by Macklin
et~al.~\cite{10.1145/2994258.2994272}, fixes this by augmenting each
constraint with a compliance term $\alpha = 1/k$ and solving for the
associated Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$. The resulting iteration solves an
implicit-Euler problem with arbitrary elastic energies, and constraint
stiffness becomes a true material parameter independent of iteration count.

\paragraph{Small steps in physics simulation.}
Macklin et~al.~\cite{Chen2024} (the ``Small Steps'' note) later argued that
XPBD converges most reliably when used with \emph{many short substeps and a
single iteration per substep}, rather than a single long step with many
iterations. This is now a standard XPBD configuration, and it is the
default we adopt in \autoref{chap:Methodology}: it both follows the
recommendation and exposes XPBD's per-substep cost as the primary
performance lever.

\section{Projective dynamics and descent methods}
\label{sec:ProjectiveDynamics}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
A parallel line of work, projective dynamics, recasts implicit Euler as a
quadratic minimisation alternating between a global linear solve and local
constraint projections. Variants and accelerations (descent methods,
Chebyshev acceleration, ADMM) bridge between projective dynamics and
position-based methods, and provide the theoretical bridge to VBD: VBD can
be read as a \emph{block-coordinate descent} on the same variational
energy~\eqref{eq:G} that projective dynamics minimises, but with single-
vertex blocks rather than a global solve.

\section{Vertex Block Descent (VBD) and successors}
\label{sec:VBDLineage}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Vertex Block Descent, due to Chen et~al.~\cite{Chen2024}, treats each
vertex as a tiny optimisation block: at each iteration, every vertex moves
to the minimiser of $G$ with all other vertices held fixed. This requires
the local $3\times 3$ Hessian of the energies sharing the vertex and a
Newton step on that block. Critical features of the original VBD paper
include:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Chebyshev acceleration}, an over-relaxation that
    significantly speeds convergence at the cost of energy guarantees.
    \item \emph{Adaptive initialisation}, which seeds the iteration from a
    blend of inertial and previous-step positions.
    \item \emph{Graph-colouring parallelism}: vertices that share no
    constraint can be updated independently, enabling massive parallelism.
\end{itemize}

A follow-up line, Augmented VBD (AVBD)~\cite{Chen2024} and 2025
extensions, has improved stiffness-ratio handling and convergence behaviour.
We restrict the present comparison to the canonical 2024 VBD since AVBD
post-dates our implementation window; we cite AVBD in
\autoref{sec:FlexResults} as the most relevant counter-evidence on the
stiffness-ratio axis.

\section{Prior comparison: Ma et~al.\ (2024)}
\label{sec:Ma2024}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
The most directly comparable prior work is Ma et~al.~\cite{app142311072},
who compare cloth simulation under PBD and VBD in Unity. Their study
established that VBD scales better than PBD with node count and identified
maximum sustainable node counts at 30~fps.

Our work differs in three substantial ways:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{XPBD, not PBD.} Plain PBD has the stiffness-coupling flaw
    that XPBD was designed to fix, so a PBD-vs-VBD comparison conflates
    algorithmic differences with this known bug. Using XPBD isolates the
    primal/dual distinction.
    \item \textbf{Canonical VBD.} Ma et~al.'s pseudocode departs in details
    (initialisation, Chebyshev handling) from the formulation in
    \cite{Chen2024}. We follow the canonical VBD and disable Chebyshev /
    adaptive init for the core comparison so that we are reading off
    algorithm properties, not acceleration tricks.
    \item \textbf{Beyond frame rate.} Ma et~al.\ report fps and node counts.
    We add a Newton ground truth, an equal-wall-clock quality frontier, and
    targeted stress tests (mass ratio, stiffness ratio, large $dt$) keyed to
    the primal/dual hypotheses.
\end{enumerate}

In short, this thesis answers a question Ma et~al.\ explicitly left as
future work: how does VBD compare to \emph{XPBD}, and at \emph{matched
quality} rather than only at matched node counts?
