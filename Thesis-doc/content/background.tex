%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\chapter{Background and theoretical foundations}
\label{chap:Background}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter establishes the mathematical scaffolding the rest of the thesis
relies on. The central object is the variational form of implicit Euler, which
unifies XPBD and VBD as two approximate minimisers of the same energy
functional, and therefore justifies a shared Newton reference as ground truth.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Time-stepping schemes for physical simulation}
\label{sec:Integration}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Real-time physics ultimately reduces to advancing a state
$(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{v})$ --- positions and velocities of $n$ particles ---
forward in time. Three families of approaches have been used in computer
graphics.

\paragraph{Newtonian, force-based.}
Forces $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{v}, t)$ produce accelerations via
$\ddot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1} \mathbf{f}$, integrated by some scheme.

\paragraph{Impulse-based.} Velocities are manipulated directly via collision
or constraint impulses, popular for rigid bodies. I will not pursue impulse
methods further: they are awkward for distributed deformation, and the
primal/dual framework of \autoref{sec:PrimalDual} is the right lens for
comparing XPBD and VBD.

\paragraph{Position-based.} Positions are manipulated directly; velocities are
recovered as finite differences. This is the lineage XPBD belongs to.

\subsection{Explicit Euler and why it fails}
\label{ssec:ExplicitEuler}
The explicit (forward) Euler scheme is
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{v}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{v}_t + h\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_t), \\
    \mathbf{x}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{x}_t + h\,\mathbf{v}_{t+h}.
\end{align}
For a linear spring with stiffness $k$ and mass $m$, stability requires the
time step to satisfy roughly $h < 2\sqrt{m/k}$. For cloth, where $k$ may
exceed $10^4$~N/m, this drives $h$ down to microseconds --- entirely
incompatible with a 16~ms real-time frame budget. Even velocity Verlet
and symplectic-Euler variants retain a stability limit driven by the stiffest
mode.

\subsection{Implicit Euler}
\label{ssec:ImplicitEuler}
The (backward) implicit Euler scheme evaluates forces at the unknown
end-of-step state:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{v}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{v}_t + h\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_{t+h}), \\
    \mathbf{x}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{x}_t + h\,\mathbf{v}_{t+h}.
\end{align}
This scheme is unconditionally stable for linear systems and dissipative for
non-linear ones, at the cost of a non-linear root-finding problem each step.

\section{Implicit Euler as energy minimisation}
\label{sec:Variational}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Eliminating the velocity equation gives the well-known variational form of
implicit Euler: the new positions $\mathbf{x}$ minimise
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:G}
    G(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2 h^{2}} \,\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|_{\mathbf{M}}^{2}
                       \;+\; E(\mathbf{x}),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{y} \;=\; \mathbf{x}_{t} + h\,\mathbf{v}_{t} + h^{2}\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{ext}}
\end{equation*}
is the \emph{inertial target} (the position the system would reach under
external forces alone), $\|\cdot\|_{\mathbf{M}}$ denotes the mass-weighted
norm, and $E(\mathbf{x})$ is the elastic potential energy.

Equation~\eqref{eq:G} is central to this thesis for one reason:
\textbf{both XPBD and VBD are approximate minimisers of the same $G(\mathbf{x})$}.
XPBD reaches a stationary point by descending on the Lagrange-dual problem;
VBD reaches one by block-coordinate descent on the primal. This shared
target is what licenses a Newton solve of \eqref{eq:G} to serve as an
unambiguous ground truth in \autoref{chap:Experiments}.

\section{Mass-spring systems}
\label{sec:MassSpring}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Throughout this work I use a mass-spring discretisation of cloth: vertices
carry lumped masses, edges of the cloth mesh carry springs. For a spring
between particles $i$ and $j$ with rest length $\ell_0$ and stiffness $k$,
the constraint function and energy are
\begin{align}
    C(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) &= \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\| - \ell_0, \\
    E(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) &= \tfrac{1}{2}\, k\, C^{2}.
\end{align}

\subsection{Distance, stretch, and bending springs}
\label{ssec:SpringTypes}
I use two spring types, both expressed through the distance-spring energy
above:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Stretch springs} between mesh-adjacent vertices oppose
    in-plane deformation.
    \item \emph{Bending springs} are modelled as long-range distance springs
    between vertices opposite across an edge (the ``diagonal'' of the
    two-triangle quad). Note this is \emph{not} a dihedral-angle constraint:
    I deliberately stay within a single constraint form, since the goal is
    a controlled comparison rather than a state-of-the-art cloth model.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Limitations of mass-spring}
\label{ssec:MSSLimits}
Mass-spring is cheap and easy to differentiate but is well known to be
\emph{mesh-dependent}: the effective macroscopic stiffness depends on the
discretisation. Conclusions about absolute material parameters are therefore
not transferable to FEM-based models; conclusions about \emph{relative}
solver behaviour --- my subject --- are.

\section{Solver structure: Gauss--Seidel vs.\ Jacobi}
\label{sec:GSJacobi}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Both XPBD's constraint sweep and VBD's vertex-block sweep are
Gauss--Seidel-style iterations: each local update uses the most recent values
of its neighbours rather than the values from the start of the iteration.
A Jacobi variant of either solver would update all blocks from the start-of-
iteration state in parallel; in serial it converges more slowly, in parallel
it maps better to the GPU.

This has an important consequence I flag now and revisit in
\autoref{sec:ThreatsValidity}: both solvers are \textbf{order-dependent}.
The constraint-traversal order in XPBD and the vertex-traversal order in VBD
both affect convergence rate and, in pathological cases, the converged
solution. My experiments fix a deterministic order for reproducibility.

\section{The primal/dual distinction}
\label{sec:PrimalDual}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
The unifying framing for this thesis is the primal/dual perspective due to
Macklin et~al.~\cite{Chen2024}. Minimising $G(\mathbf{x})$ in
\eqref{eq:G} can be approached in two ways:

\paragraph{Primal.} Directly minimise over the positions $\mathbf{x}$.
A vertex-by-vertex block-coordinate descent on $G$ is exactly VBD.

\paragraph{Dual.} Reformulate the energy through compliant constraints
(treating each spring as a soft constraint with compliance
$\alpha = 1/k$) and solve for Lagrange multipliers $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ on
each constraint. XPBD is a Gauss--Seidel iteration on the resulting
saddle-point system.

This duality predicts the hypotheses stated in \autoref{sec:Hypotheses}:
the dual variables couple constraints sharing a particle, so mass
disparities at a shared particle hurt XPBD (H1); the primal Hessian block at
a vertex collects all spring stiffnesses meeting there, so widely varying
stiffnesses produce an ill-conditioned block and hurt VBD (H2). The
mechanism for stiffness-robust per-iteration convergence in VBD (H3) follows
from the primal Hessian containing the true elastic operator, while the
constraint linearisation underlying XPBD discards second-order information.
