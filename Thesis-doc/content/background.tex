%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\chapter{Background and theoretical foundations}
\label{chap:Background}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------

This chapter establishes the mathematical foundations for the rest of the
thesis. Its central object is the variational form of implicit Euler, which
unifies XPBD and VBD as two approximate minimizers of the same energy function
and therefore justifies a shared Newton solver as ground truth. The theoretical
treatment follows Li et~al.~\cite{li2026physics}, which gives detailed
descriptions and case studies of the theory and algorithms across most areas of
physics-based simulation.

%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Time-stepping schemes for physical simulation}
\label{sec:Integration}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
At its core, real-time physics reduces to advancing a state
$(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{v})$ --- the positions and velocities of $n$ particles ---
forward in time. Modern physics engines can be grouped into three families
according to how they resolve constraints and compute motion: force-based,
impulse-based, and position-based methods.

\paragraph{Force-based (Newtonian).}
Forces $\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{v}, t)$ produce accelerations through
Newton's second law, $\mathbf{f} = \mathbf{M}\,\tfrac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt}$ with
$\mathbf{v} = \tfrac{d\mathbf{x}}{dt}$, which rearranges to
$\ddot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}$, where $\mathbf{M}$ is the mass
matrix. This is the most intuitive approach. It is largely obsolete for real-time
applications but is still used where high precision is required, such as
engineering problems and offline visual effects.

\paragraph{Impulse-based.}
Velocities are manipulated directly through collision or constraint impulses.
This family is popular for rigid bodies because it handles collisions as
instantaneous changes in velocity. I do not pursue it here, since instantaneous
velocity changes are not well suited to deformable bodies.

\paragraph{Position-based.}
Constraints are resolved by directly updating positions, without explicitly
computing forces or accelerations. PBD and XPBD belong to this category. VBD is a
hybrid: mathematically it is a position-based solver, since it directly updates
positions, but it does so by solving a local optimization problem that mimics a
force-based solver, so it is force-based in spirit as well.

\subsection{Explicit time integration (forward Euler) and why it fails}
\label{ssec:ExplicitEuler}
The scheme is called explicit because it computes $\mathbf{x}_{t+h}$ and
$\mathbf{v}_{t+h}$ directly, by substituting known values into simple formulas:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{v}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{v}_t + h\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_t), \\
    \mathbf{x}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{x}_t + h\,\mathbf{v}_{t+h}.
\end{align}
Here $\mathbf{v}_t$ and $\mathbf{x}_t$ are the velocity and position at time $t$,
and $h$ is the time step. The method is considered unconditionally unstable:
regardless of $h$, the simulation will eventually explode for equations with a
nonconstant $\mathbf{f}$.

\subsection{Implicit time integration (backward Euler)}
\label{ssec:ImplicitEuler}
Implicit time integration, in contrast, requires solving a system of equations to
determine $\mathbf{x}_{t+h}$ and $\mathbf{v}_{t+h}$. The backward Euler scheme
evaluates forces at the unknown end-of-step state:
\begin{align}
    \mathbf{v}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{v}_t + h\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}_{t+h}), \\
    \mathbf{x}_{t+h} &= \mathbf{x}_t + h\,\mathbf{v}_{t+h}.
\end{align}
Now $\mathbf{v}_{t+h}$ depends on $\mathbf{x}_{t+h}$, and $\mathbf{x}_{t+h}$
depends on $\mathbf{v}_{t+h}$. This circular dependency is resolved by
substituting the estimate
$\mathbf{v}_{t+h} = (\mathbf{x}_{t+h} - \mathbf{x}_t)/h$ into the position-update
equation. The scheme is unconditionally stable for linear systems, but it may
fail to converge if the initial approximation is far from the true solution, and
it carries a real cost: a nonlinear root-finding problem must be solved at every
step.

\section{Implicit Euler as energy minimization}
\label{sec:Variational}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Eliminating the velocity equation yields the well-known variational form (also
called the minimization or optimization form) of implicit Euler: $\mathbf{x}$ is
the minimizer of an energy function $G$, so that
$\mathbf{x}_{t+h} = \argmin_{\mathbf{x}} G(\mathbf{x})$, with
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:G}
    G(\mathbf{x}) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2 h^{2}} \,\|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|_{\mathbf{M}}^{2}
                       \;+\; E(\mathbf{x}),
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation*}
    \mathbf{y} \;=\; \mathbf{x}_{t} + h\,\mathbf{v}_{t} + h^{2}\,\mathbf{M}^{-1}\mathbf{f}_{\mathrm{ext}}.
\end{equation*}
Here $\mathbf{y}$ is the \emph{inertial target} (the position reached under
external forces alone), $\|\cdot\|_{\mathbf{M}}$ is the mass-weighted length, and
$E(\mathbf{x})$ is the total potential energy evaluated at $\mathbf{x}$.

Equation~\eqref{eq:G} is central to the thesis, because \textbf{both XPBD and VBD
are approximate minimizers of the same $G(\mathbf{x})$}. They differ only in
\emph{which unknowns} they optimize over. VBD minimizes $G$ directly over the
positions $\mathbf{x}$ --- the \emph{primal} variables --- by block-coordinate
descent. XPBD instead rewrites the elastic energy as a set of compliant
constraints and solves for the Lagrange multipliers (the constraint forces) that
enforce them --- the \emph{dual} variables. This is precisely what makes one a
primal and the other a dual method; \autoref{sec:PrimalDual} develops the
distinction and what it predicts. Because both target the same minimizer, a
Newton solve of \eqref{eq:G} can serve as an unambiguous ground truth in
\autoref{chap:Experiments}.

\section{Mass-spring systems}
\label{sec:MassSpring}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
I use a mass-spring discretization of cloth: vertices carry point masses, and the
edges of the cloth mesh carry springs. For a spring between particles $i$ and $j$
with rest length $\ell_0$ and stiffness $k$, the constraint function and the
associated energy are
\begin{align}
    C(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) &= \|\mathbf{x}_i - \mathbf{x}_j\| - \ell_0, \\
    E(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j) &= \tfrac{1}{2}\, k\, C^{2}.
\end{align}
The exact mass-spring structure used to represent cloth is introduced in
\autoref{ssec:ClothModel}.

\subsection{Limitations of mass-spring models}
\label{ssec:MSSLimits}
The mass-spring model is cheap and easy to differentiate, but it is well known to
be \emph{simulation-mesh-dependent}: effective material parameters, such as
macroscopic stiffness, depend on the discretization. Conclusions about absolute
material parameters therefore do not transfer to FEM-based models. Conclusions
about \emph{relative} solver behavior --- my actual subject --- do transfer.

\section{Solver structure: Gauss--Seidel vs.\ Jacobi}
\label{sec:GSJacobi}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Both XPBD's constraint sweep and VBD's vertex-block sweep are Gauss--Seidel-style
iterations: each local update uses the most recent neighbor values rather than
the start-of-iteration values. A Jacobi variant, by contrast, updates all blocks
in parallel from the start-of-iteration state; it converges more slowly in serial
execution but maps better to the GPU.

A consequence, revisited in \autoref{sec:ThreatsValidity}, is that both solvers
are \textbf{order-dependent}. The constraint-traversal order (XPBD) and the
vertex-traversal order (VBD) both affect the convergence rate and, in some
relatively rare cases, the converged solution itself. My experiments fix a
deterministic order for reproducibility; I do not use randomization, since none
of the canonical solver implementations do.

\section{The primal/dual distinction}
\label{sec:PrimalDual}
%----------------------------------------------------------------------------
The unifying framing of this thesis is the primal/dual perspective of Macklin
et~al.~\cite{10.1111/cgf.14104}. Minimizing $G(\mathbf{x})$ in \eqref{eq:G} can be
approached in two ways.

\paragraph{Primal.}
The energy is minimized directly over the positions $\mathbf{x}$. A
vertex-by-vertex block-coordinate descent on $G$ is exactly VBD.

\paragraph{Dual.}
The energy is reformulated through compliant constraints --- each spring becomes a
soft constraint with compliance $\alpha = 1/k$ --- and the solver works for the
Lagrange multipliers $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ on those constraints. Introducing the
multipliers turns the pure minimization over $\mathbf{x}$ into a coupled problem
in both $\mathbf{x}$ and $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$: a \emph{saddle-point} (or KKT)
system, so called because its solution minimizes the energy in the position
variables while maximizing it in the multipliers, rather than minimizing in all
unknowns at once. XPBD solves this system with a Gauss--Seidel sweep, updating one
constraint's multiplier --- and the positions it touches --- at a time.

This duality is the thesis's motivation for choosing the stress tests of
\autoref{ssec:ChainWithHeavyWeight} and \autoref{ssec:StiffnessRatioChain},
because it also reveals where the two approaches naturally differ. Dual variables
couple constraints that share a particle, so a mass disparity at a shared particle
is a scenario in which XPBD's per-constraint updates may propagate slowly. The
primal Hessian block at a vertex, on the other hand, collects all spring
stiffnesses meeting there, so widely varying stiffnesses produce an
ill-conditioned block --- a scenario in which VBD's per-vertex update may struggle.
